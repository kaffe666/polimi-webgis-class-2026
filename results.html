Results

The results present the final maps and charts generated for each pollutant. The interpretation focuses on spatial concentration patterns, annual change, land cover transition statistics and population exposure. Use the tabs to switch between NO₂, PM2.5 and PM10.

NO₂ Concentration in Greece, December 2023

The December 2023 NO₂ map shows an uneven distribution across Greece. Higher values appear around major urban and transport-related regions, especially the Athens metropolitan area and parts of northern Greece, while many island and rural regions show lower values. This monthly snapshot precedes the annual average calculation.

Annual Average NO₂ Concentration, 2023

The annual average map highlights the stable spatial pattern of NO₂ in 2023. Higher values concentrate around urbanised areas, while lower values spread across rural and island regions. Compared with the December map, the annual average smooths monthly fluctuations and is better suited to classification and exposure analysis.

NO₂ Concentration Classes, 2023

The concentration class map divides annual average NO₂ into air quality classes. Greece mainly contains Class 1, Class 2 and Class 3 values, with most of the country in Class 1, indicating relatively low NO₂ levels. Class 2 and Class 3 areas are more limited and are mainly associated with urban regions.

NO₂ Annual Mean Aggregation Change, 2021–2023

The NO₂ AMAC map shows both increases and decreases between 2021 and 2023. Some regions show slight or moderate increases while others remain stable or decline. Stronger changes appear in localised zones rather than across the whole country, reflecting differences in urban activity, transport intensity and regional emission patterns.

Built Area Change and NO₂ Change Statistics

The built area change map identifies Stable Built Area, Built Area Gain and Built Area Loss. Most built-up pixels are stable, while gain and loss areas are scattered. The bar chart shows Built Area Loss with the highest mean NO₂ change (about 1.173 µg/m³), while Stable Built Area carries the highest maximum value, suggesting persistent urban areas experienced strong localised NO₂ increases.

NO₂–Population Bivariate Exposure

The bivariate map combines NO₂ concentration class and population class at the GAUL Level 2 scale. The first digit represents the NO₂ class, the second the population class. Visible combinations include 13, 14, 23 and 35; the Class 35 area is especially important because it combines a higher NO₂ class with a high population class.

Population Exposure to NO₂ Concentration Classes

The exposure pie chart shows 36.24% of the population in Class 1, 26.81% in Class 2 and 36.96% in Class 3. Although much of the territory is mapped as low concentration, a considerable share of the population falls into higher NO₂ exposure classes, demonstrating why population-weighted exposure analysis is necessary in addition to simple spatial mapping.

PM2.5 Concentration in Greece, December 2023

The December 2023 PM2.5 map shows higher concentrations around major urban and transport-related areas, especially Athens and parts of northern Greece, while many island and rural regions show lower values. Short-term PM2.5 is strongly influenced by urban activity, traffic intensity and regional emission sources.

Annual Average PM2.5 Concentration, 2023

The annual average map shows the highest values concentrated around large urban areas, especially the Athens region, with lower values in less urbanised mainland areas and many islands. The annual average smooths short-term fluctuations and highlights the clear contrast between urbanised and non-urbanised areas.

PM2.5 Concentration Classes, 2023

The class map shows mainly Class 1, Class 2 and Class 3, with most of the country in Class 2 and Class 3 (between 5 and 20 µg/m³). Class 1 areas are limited, mainly around specific islands and high-altitude ecosystems. This classification provides the pollutant input for the bivariate exposure map.

PM2.5 Annual Mean Aggregation Change, 2021–2023

The PM2.5 AMAC map shows both increases and decreases. Western Greece forms a distinct increase hotspot, while eastern administrative regions and coastal plains show neutral to stabilising trends (0 to −1.5 µg/m³). The change is spatially uneven, showing not only where PM2.5 was high in 2023 but where conditions shifted since 2021.

PM2.5 Change Statistics by Crop Area Transition

Using crop area transitions as zones, Stable Crop Area shows a mean PM2.5 change of about 0.226 µg/m³, Crop Area Gain about 0.088 µg/m³, and Crop Area Loss a negative mean of −0.101 µg/m³. PM2.5 grew least in newly gained crop cover, a relative buffering signature, while all classes share local bounds from −4.392 to +4.717 µg/m³.

Crop Gain and Loss Percentage and Area Statistics

The crop gain and loss statistics summarise how much agricultural land was gained or lost between 2021 and 2023 across different transition categories. The chart presents both the percentage share and the absolute area for each transition type, giving a quantitative basis for interpreting the PM2.5 zonal statistics above.

PM2.5–Population Bivariate Exposure

The bivariate map combines PM2.5 class and population class at the administrative zone level. Visible combinations include 33, 34 and 35: several regions share a baseline Class 3 pollution level but differ in demographic weight. The Class 35 areas are especially important, capturing critical urban centres such as Athens.

Population Exposure to PM2.5 Concentration Classes

The exposure chart shows essentially the entire calculated population in Class 3 (greater than 10 and up to 20 µg/m³), because this regulatory tier covers the populated provincial zones uniformly. Even though much of the territory maps as lower concentration, population-weighted analysis reveals widespread exposure to the higher class.

PM10 Concentration in Greece, December 2023

The December 2023 PM10 map shows higher values mainly in northern and central Greece, where urban heating, road traffic, agriculture and dust resuspension raise winter levels. Thessaloniki and the Thessaly plain stand out, while coastal and island areas benefit from sea-breeze dispersion.

Annual Average PM10 Concentration, 2023

The annual average map concentrates the highest values in northern Greece, around Thessaloniki, the Thessaly plain and Central Macedonia, driven by industry, traffic, construction and agricultural burning. The Greater Athens area is also elevated, while southern Greece and the Aegean islands generally show lower annual averages.

PM10 Concentration Classes, 2023

The class map shows mainly Class 1, Class 2 and Class 3, with Class 4 and Class 5 nearly absent. Class 1 covers mountainous, island and southern coastal areas; Class 2 dominates mid-sized urban zones and agricultural lowlands; and Class 3 appears in the most urbanised and industrialised areas around Thessaloniki and Greater Athens.

PM10 Annual Mean Aggregation Change, 2021–2023

The PM10 AMAC map shows increases in parts of northern and central Greece, linked to expanded construction, new road infrastructure and agricultural land disturbance, while parts of Attica and several islands show slight decreases. The pattern is spatially heterogeneous, driven by local activity and land use rather than a uniform national trend.

PM10 Zonal Statistics by Built Area Transition

Using built area transitions as zones, Built Area Gain shows the highest mean PM10 change, reflecting increased particulate matter from ground disturbance and construction machinery. Stable Built Area shows a moderate mean change, while Built Area Loss shows the smallest or slightly negative mean, consistent with reduced dust generation where hard surfaces were removed.

PM10–Population Bivariate Exposure

The bivariate map combines PM10 class and population class. Zones high in both dimensions mark the greatest public-health concern and cluster around Thessaloniki and metropolitan Athens. Northern peripheral zones show elevated PM10 but smaller populations, while rural and island zones are low in both dimensions.
